Modular arithmetic
Modular arithmetic is a very efficient and neat way in finding the remainders of certain quotients and the properties of certain numbers. As you can see in the example on the right, there are three values concerning the modulus operator. Firstly, the value infront of the operator represents the value being divided, with the value on the right of the operator being the divisor, and the final value on the other side of the equation being the remainder. For example, 5 mod 2 would equal 1 as the remainder of 5 divided by 2 would be one.

As you have seen in the example above, the modulus function can be represented using an equal sign. However, most of the time, the modulus function is used using a congruence sign. This is because of congruence sign does not mean that both sides must be exactly equal, but that they can be expressed as one another. This is useful in many cases, but here is an example. 5 mod 2 is congruent to both 1 and 3, as you can choose to divide 5 by either 2 once or twice to receive the remainders 1 or 3. This use of the congruence symbol is used in the basic arithmetic for modular functions described below and will be helpful in other parts of this lesson.
